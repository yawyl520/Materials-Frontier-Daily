## [Layer-Engineering Enables Switchable 2D Altermagnetism](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31241) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Researchers propose a universal layer-engineering paradigm that sandwiches an antiferromagnetic monolayer between two ferroelectric layers to achieve symmetry-enforced ferroelectric switching of two-dimensional altermagnetism, validated with first-principles calculations on the In2Se3/MnPTe3/In2Se3 trilayer. This work resolves a fundamental bottleneck in altermagnetism—nonvolatile deterministic switching—enabling electrically addressable altermagnetic spintronics without stringent symmetry constraints, potentially advancing spintronics and materials science. The mechanism relies on global combined parity-time symmetry, where reversing ferroelectric polarization inverts the altermagnetic spin-splitting pattern, deterministically flipping the anomalous Hall effect signal as a transport fingerprint. The method is universal and not limited to intrinsic single-phase materials.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: Altermagnetism is a magnetic state with zero net magnetization but strong momentum-dependent spin splitting, offering a platform for spintronics. However, nonvolatile deterministic switching between its two equivalent spin-splitting states has been a major challenge. Ferroelectric materials can have their polarization reversed by an electric field, providing a potential route for electrical control. This work combines these concepts through a layer-engineering approach.

## [Atom diffraction enters strong-coupling regime in graphene](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31183) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Helium atom diffraction on freestanding single-layer graphene at kiloelectronvolt energies reveals a non-perturbative strong-coupling regime, where lattice distortions induce phase shifts of several radians that cannot be described by the conventional Debye-Waller factor. This finding challenges the foundational perturbative approach in matter-wave diffraction, potentially altering how phonon effects are modeled in condensed-matter research and enabling more accurate material characterization. The strong-coupling regime arises from the projectile interacting simultaneously with the electron density of multiple lattice atoms; in contrast, atomic hydrogen diffraction retains the weak-coupling regime. Simulations support the experimental results and provide a regime-independent approach to describe phonon influences.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: In condensed-matter research, atom diffraction is used to probe material properties via diffraction patterns, typically assuming that lattice vibrations cause only weak perturbations described by the Debye-Waller factor. The Debye-Waller factor quantifies the attenuation of diffraction intensity due to thermal motion and is a standard perturbative tool. This work shows that for helium on graphene at keV energies, the interaction becomes so strong that the perturbative treatment fails, revealing a new strong-coupling regime where phase shifts are large and the effective diffraction lattice changes from atomic sites to hexagon centers.

## [Non-Hermitian Skyrmion's Topological Charge Splits at Exceptional Points](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27810) ⭐️ 9.0/10

This paper reveals that in non-Hermitian skyrmions with PT-symmetric gain and loss, the topological protection splits into two distinct charges: a homotopy-protected right-state charge and a biorthogonal charge that breaks down at exceptional points. This work fundamentally challenges the conventional understanding of topological protection in magnetism, showing that protection is not a single property in non-Hermitian systems. It opens new avenues for exploring non-Hermitian topology in real-space magnetic textures and could impact future spintronic devices. The non-Hermitian skyrmion carries two topological charges: one from the right eigenstate (homotopy-protected) and one from the biorthogonal left-right pair (complex-valued and non-quantized). The biorthogonal charge breaks down at an exceptional point located on a ring around the skyrmion's equator, where the biorthogonal Bloch field diverges.

rss · arXiv Mesoscale and Nanoscale Physics · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: Skyrmions are topologically protected magnetic textures that carry an integer topological charge, making them robust against perturbations. Non-Hermitian physics, involving open systems with gain and loss, introduces exceptional points where eigenvalues and eigenvectors coalesce. This paper merges these concepts by considering a skyrmion in a magnet with PT-symmetric gain and loss, leading to a splitting of topological protection.

This resolves a long-standing open problem in frustrated magnetism by providing clear evidence for a quantum spin liquid phase and demonstrating the role of symmetry in PEPS simulations, significantly advancing our understanding of the triangular lattice J1-J2 model. ## [Ultrafast fluence reversal fingerprints fragile Kondo hybridization](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31361) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Using femtosecond pump-probe reflectivity, researchers discovered a fluence-reversal fingerprint in CePt2In7 that reveals the Kondo hybridization gap is a fragile, dynamically reconfigurable many-body state rather than a rigid electronic structure. The ultrafast optical signature shows that the electronic response transitions from weak-linear behavior to Rothwarf-Taylor bottleneck suppression and then to anomalous enhancement at the lowest temperatures. This finding challenges the long-held assumption of a rigid Kondo hybridization gap in heavy-fermion materials, providing new insights into quantum criticality and unconventional superconductivity. The work highlights ultrafast spectroscopy as a powerful tool to probe fragile many-body electronic states. In CePt2In7, a weakly hybridized Kondo lattice, the low-energy recombination scale is 2Δ ≈ 7.4 meV with a crossover temperature T* ≈ 40 K. Coherent optical phonons identified via Raman spectroscopy exclude significant lattice heating, and the phonon-free electronic amplitude A_elec shows a clear fluence-reversal fingerprint: from weak-linear to Rothwarf-Taylor bottleneck suppression to anomalous enhancement.

rss · arXiv Strongly Correlated Electrons · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: Kondo lattice systems feature a hybridization gap that forms between conduction electrons and localized f-electrons, giving rise to heavy quasiparticles. The rigidity of this gap is a key question in heavy-fermion physics. Ultrafast pump-probe reflectivity measures the transient change in reflectivity after a laser pulse, revealing carrier dynamics. The Rothwarf-Taylor model describes a bottleneck in carrier recombination across a gap when phonon coupling is weak.
