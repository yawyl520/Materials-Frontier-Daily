## [AI materials proposals break structural memory of experiments](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30967) ⭐️ 9.0/10

A new study embedding 167,500 Inorganic Crystal Structure Database (ICSD) entries in a structural similarity space reveals that experimental materials discovery exhibits strong structural memory, while AI-generated proposals (GNoME, MatterGen) show significant departure from experimentally realized structures. This provides the first quantitative reference frame to evaluate generative models in materials science, highlighting a critical gap between computational predictions and experimental reality that could reshape how we prioritize candidates for synthesis. The study finds 82.9% of new formulas enter pre-existing structural communities; new-community formation dropped from 40.2% in the 1930s to 2.6% in the 2010s. The computed sets rank: held-out ICSD > MatterGen > {GNoME ~ Materials Project theoretical} > JARVIS-DFT > Alexandria-PBE, showing departure is general across computational methods.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: The Inorganic Crystal Structure Database (ICSD) contains experimentally determined crystal structures. Generative AI models like GNoME and MatterGen propose novel structures by learning from known data, but they often predict structures that do not resemble those found in nature, raising questions about synthesizability. This study uses structural similarity networks and historical analysis to quantify that gap.

## [Symmetry-Enforced Ferroelectric Switching of 2D Altermagnetism](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31241) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Researchers propose a layer-engineering paradigm that uses ferroelectric/antiferromagnetic heterostructures to achieve deterministic, nonvolatile switching of altermagnetic spin splitting via polarization reversal, validated by first-principles calculations in an In2Se3/MnPTe3/In2Se3 trilayer. This work addresses a key bottleneck in altermagnetism – nonvolatile switching – and provides an electrically controllable framework for altermagnetic spintronics, potentially enabling energy-efficient memory and logic devices. The mechanism relies on global combined parity-time (PT) symmetry: reversing ferroelectric polarization exactly inverts the altermagnetic spin-splitting pattern, which deterministically flips the anomalous Hall effect signal as a transport fingerprint.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: Altermagnetism is a recently discovered magnetic order with zero net magnetization but strong momentum-dependent spin splitting, promising for spintronics. However, switching between its two equivalent spin-splitting states nonvolatilely has been difficult. This work introduces a heterostructure approach that bypasses the stringent symmetry requirements of intrinsic single-phase altermagnets.

## [Bichromatic Floquet Driving Activates Giant Edelstein Polarization in Altermagnets](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31867) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Researchers demonstrate that bichromatic Floquet driving combined with Rashba spin-orbit coupling can activate giant perpendicular Edelstein polarization (0.5–1.5 μB) in 2D altermagnets and other unconventional magnets, which is impossible with monochromatic driving. This discovery provides a stray-field-free, all-optical mechanism for perpendicular memory writing in spintronics, overcoming fundamental symmetry constraints that previously prevented such responses in d-wave altermagnets. The bichromatic driving dynamically breaks two-fold rotational symmetry, generating an in-plane Zeeman-like field that induces orbitally dominated polarization with universal selection rules based on magnetic parity and harmonics.

rss · arXiv Mesoscale and Nanoscale Physics · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: Altermagnets are a class of collinear magnets with zero net magnetization but spin-split electronic bands, unlike conventional antiferromagnets. The Edelstein effect typically describes current-induced spin polarization in systems with spin-orbit coupling. Floquet engineering uses periodic driving (such as lasers) to modify material properties; bichromatic driving employs two frequencies to achieve effects not possible with a single frequency.

## [Intrinsic Anderson Localization Observed in Few-Layer ReS2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31233) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Researchers report the first direct observation of intrinsic Anderson localization in few-layer ReS2 by using all-dry van der Waals assembly and an hBN gate dielectric to eliminate extrinsic disorder. Temperature-dependent transport reveals a crossover to 2D Mott variable-range hopping with a localization length of (3.5±0.1) nm. This observation confirms a fundamental quantum phenomenon in a 2D material system with minimal disorder, providing a platform to study intrinsic electron localization. It also demonstrates a clean fabrication method that could enable more reliable transport measurements in other van der Waals materials. The study uses few-layer ReS2, a material with narrow conduction bandwidth, and achieves a localization length an order of magnitude larger than disorder-dominated devices. The non-monotonic gate-voltage dependence of activation energy directly accesses the energy-resolved band-tail density of states of the ReS2 conduction band.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: Anderson localization is the absence of diffusion of waves in a disordered medium, named after Philip W. Anderson. For electrons in 2D, disorder can lead to localization, but extrinsic fabrication-induced disorder often masks intrinsic behavior. ReS2 is a 2D semiconductor with a unique distorted 1T structure and narrow conduction bandwidth, making it particularly susceptible to electron localization. Dry van der Waals assembly and hBN dielectrics are techniques used to create ultraclean interfaces in 2D heterostructures.

## [Contrastive Regularization Fixes Sampling Failure of Machine Learning Potentials](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31660) ⭐️ 9.0/10

The paper introduces Contrastive Regularized MSE (CRMSE), a post-training method that adds a contrastive term from KL divergence to correct the Boltzmann distribution of machine learning potentials, fixing their tendency to drift into spurious low-energy minima during sampling. This work addresses a critical flaw in machine learning potentials: pointwise accuracy on energies and forces does not guarantee correct thermodynamic observables when used as samplers. The CRMSE method could significantly improve the reliability of AI-driven molecular simulations across materials science and chemistry. CRMSE uses persistent Langevin chains to generate configurations the potential erroneously favors and raises their energy, requiring no new DFT data. On the MD17 ethanol and aspirin molecules, it recovers energy distributions, interatomic-distance distributions, and dihedral free-energy profiles to near-quantitative agreement with DFT, while preserving force accuracy.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: Machine learning interatomic potentials (MLPs) are fast surrogate models for ab initio methods like DFT, trained to predict energies and forces. However, when used to drive molecular dynamics simulations, the potential defines an implicit Boltzmann distribution; standard MSE training does not align this distribution with the true one, causing sampling to drift into unphysical states.

## [Long-range frustration boosts skyrmion stability beyond micromagnetic limits](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31633) ⭐️ 9.0/10

A new study demonstrates that long-range exchange frustration can significantly increase the energy barrier against skyrmion collapse without enlarging skyrmion size or increasing the magnetic energy scale, breaking the conventional micromagnetic scaling paradigm. This discovery challenges the fundamental assumptions of micromagnetic models used for spintronic device design, and introduces long-range frustration engineering as a new route to realize highly stable nanoscale skyrmions, which is crucial for future memory and logic technologies. By mapping a continuum model onto a spin-lattice Hamiltonian, the authors found that skyrmions with identical micromagnetic parameters can have different energy barriers due to underlying atomistic exchange interactions. They developed an exchange optimization framework, predicting that long-range frustration can double the energy barrier in physically realistic conditions relevant to ultrathin films or van der Waals magnets.

rss · arXiv Mesoscale and Nanoscale Physics · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: Magnetic skyrmions are nanoscale whirls of spin textures that are promising for spintronic applications due to their stability and low current-driven motion. In conventional micromagnetics, the skyrmion stability, quantified by the collapse energy barrier, scales with skyrmion size or exchange stiffness. Exchange frustration arises when competing exchange interactions prevent a simple magnetic order, and long-range frustration extends beyond nearest neighbors.

## [Algebraic framework explains strange metal and LFL-HFL transition](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31810) ⭐️ 9.0/10

This paper proposes an algebraic framework based on the exceptional superconformal algebra D(2,1;α) to describe the strange metal phase and the transition between Landau-Fermi liquid (LFL) and Hubbard-Fermi liquid (HFL). It derives a parameter-free prediction relating the Sommerfeld coefficient to spin and charge susceptibilities. This work provides a unified theoretical framework for the enigmatic strange metal phase observed in strongly correlated electron systems, such as high-temperature superconductors. The testable prediction offers a direct route for experimental verification, potentially advancing our understanding of non-Fermi liquid behavior. The framework employs two distinct contractions of the conformal sector of D(2,1;α): one yields canonical fermions (LFL), the other Hubbard operators (HFL). The LFL-HFL transition is found to be discontinuous at low temperatures due to a degeneracy at the emergence of HFL.

rss · arXiv Strongly Correlated Electrons · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: The strange metal phase is characterized by resistivity that scales linearly with temperature, deviating from the standard Fermi liquid theory. Landau-Fermi liquid theory describes normal metals, while the Hubbard model is a key model for strongly correlated electrons. The exceptional superconformal algebra D(2,1;α) is a mathematical structure with N=6 supersymmetry, used here as a novel tool.

## [QMC reveals symmetry-equivalent heavy-fermion and Kondo-breakdown metals](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.17801) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Using sign-problem-free quantum Monte Carlo simulations, researchers identified two symmetry-equivalent phases in a compact U(1) lattice gauge model: a heavy-fermion metal with sharp composite-fermion resonance and robust transport, and a Kondo-breakdown metal with incoherent resonance and vanishing low-frequency transport. This work provides a nonperturbative demonstration of gauge-mediated Kondo breakdown and establishes transport fingerprints of an orbital-selective Mott transition, directly relevant to understanding heavy fermion systems and strongly correlated electron materials. The model couples a spin chain to two-dimensional Dirac conduction electrons, with the spin chain described by fermionic partons. The heavy-fermion phase's Luttinger volume counts both composite and conduction electrons, whereas the Kondo-breakdown phase counts only conduction electrons, confirmed by distinct optical conductivity and spin dynamics.

rss · arXiv Strongly Correlated Electrons · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: Heavy fermion materials feature localized f-electrons that hybridize with conduction electrons via the Kondo effect. Kondo breakdown refers to a quantum phase transition where the Kondo singlet collapses, leading to a jump in Fermi surface volume. Compact U(1) lattice gauge theories are used to describe fractionalized excitations in such systems.

Researchers experimentally observed a discrete time crystal with period tripling (3T) on a trapped-ion qudit quantum processor, demonstrating a prethermal phase stabilized by interactions. This is the first realization of time-crystal behavior beyond period doubling using qudits. This work extends time-crystalline behavior to a new regime, enabling the study of subharmonic responses with higher periods and many-body entanglement. It opens avenues for quantum simulation of exotic non-equilibrium phases of matter using qudit platforms. The experiment employs a one-dimensional, disorder-free S=1 Floquet model with short-range interactions on a trapped-ion qudit processor. Period tripling is observed in local observables and spin correlations, and the prethermal phase is analyzed via effective Floquet Hamiltonians and numerical simulations.

rss · arXiv Strongly Correlated Electrons · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: A discrete time crystal is a non-equilibrium phase of matter that breaks discrete time-translation symmetry, exhibiting robust subharmonic oscillations under periodic driving. Previously, only period doubling (2T) had been observed in qubit-based systems. Qudits, with more than two levels, allow for higher-period time crystals. The prethermal phase refers to a long-lived metastable regime before thermalization, where novel phases can exist.

This paper presents a self-consistent workflow that adaptively fine-tunes a foundation machine learning interatomic potential using iterative DFT labeling, enabling efficient crystal structure prediction. Applied to the Ca-Fe-Ni system, it discovers a new high-pressure compound Ca6FeNi stable above 100 GPa. The method significantly reduces computational cost of crystal structure prediction by avoiding redundant DFT calculations while maintaining accuracy. It demonstrates a practical path for discovering new materials in complex multicomponent systems, especially under high-pressure conditions. The workflow combines evolutionary search with adaptive data selection, starting from a pretrained MLIP. It reproduces the known low-pressure convex hull of Ca-Fe-Ni and successfully predicts an unreported Ca6FeNi compound becoming stable above 100 GPa.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jul 1, 04:00

**Background**: Crystal structure prediction typically relies on repeated DFT relaxations, which are computationally expensive. Machine learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs) can accelerate this process, but require large training sets. Foundation models pretrained on diverse systems can be fine-tuned for specific tasks, but choosing which structures to label with DFT remains challenging. This work introduces an adaptive fine-tuning strategy that selects representative structures, reducing data needs.
