## [Chiral Phonons Enable Ultrafast Magnetization Switching](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17124) ⭐️ 9.0/10

A new study demonstrates that terahertz-driven chiral phonons can switch magnetization faster than linear phonons via purely magnetoelastic coupling, with the handedness of phonons controlling angular momentum transfer. This work reveals phonon chirality as a crucial parameter for ultrafast spintronics, potentially enabling faster and more energy-efficient data storage devices by eliminating electrical currents. Only one phonon handedness efficiently transfers angular momentum, explained by a large fictitious kinematic Barnett-like field at THz frequencies. Two switching regimes are identified: near the Γ-point (strong energy transfer) and near the P-point (minimal energy loss).

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jun 17, 04:00

**Background**: Chiral phonons are lattice vibrations with circular motion that carry angular momentum, distinct from linear phonons. Magnetoelastic coupling describes the interaction between magnetic order and lattice strain, enabling spin manipulation via phonons. This study combines both concepts to achieve ultrafast magnetization switching without electrical currents.

## [Hydrogen s-electrons enable ferromagnetism in predicted crystal](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17902) ⭐️ 9.0/10

First-principles calculations predict a ferromagnetic crystal H13@(BN)12 where magnetism arises from the 1s electron of a central hydrogen atom via multicenter bonding, without requiring spin-orbit coupling. This discovery challenges the long-held paradigm that magnetism requires d, f, or p electrons, opening a path to lightweight magnetic materials with low energy consumption. The central hydrogen atom retains a magnetic moment of 1 μB, and long-range magnetic order is established through multicenter bonding within the H13 aggregate and intercell B-B network. Under 16 GPa compression, the system becomes nonmagnetic metallic.

rss · arXiv Strongly Correlated Electrons · Jun 17, 04:00

**Background**: Magnetism typically arises from localized d, f, or p electrons with strong correlations; s electrons, like hydrogen's 1s, usually have their spins quenched. Chemical precompression refers to the reduction of pressure required for metallization in hydrogen-rich compounds due to alloying elements. Multicenter bonding involves electrons shared among more than two atoms, common in boranes.

Traditional exfoliation methods often require surfactants, chemical oxidation, or multiple steps, limiting scalability and purity.

The study focuses on the ν=2/5 fractional quantum Hall state, which hosts multiple edge modes. ## [Exact Stop-Band Formula via Mirror Symmetry Decomposition](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17265) ⭐️ 8.0/10

The authors derive an exact formula for stop-band intervals in periodic structures with symmetric unit cells by decomposing the eigenproblem into four quarter-cell sub-problems, avoiding full dispersion diagram computation. This method significantly simplifies the evaluation of stop-bands in phononic crystals and similar periodic structures, enabling faster and more efficient design of acoustic and elastic wave devices. The method is validated on a phononic crystal of lead cylinders in epoxy and a lattice of coupled C-shaped Helmholtz resonators, achieving 1% accuracy for the lowest bands by solving eigenvalues at only three wavevectors. Avoided crossings can cause approximate pairing, but conditions for such behavior are identified.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jun 17, 04:00

**Background**: Periodic structures like phononic crystals exhibit stop-bands (band gaps) where wave propagation is forbidden. Traditionally, computing these bands requires full dispersion diagram solving via plane-wave expansion or finite-element methods, which is computationally expensive. Mirror symmetries in the unit cell can be exploited to reduce the problem size, and this paper presents an exact decomposition using representation theory of little groups.

## [Unified framework bridging continuum and kinetic heat transport via generalized Knudsen numbers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17829) ⭐️ 8.0/10

This paper presents a unified framework that systematically reduces the linearized Peierls-Boltzmann equation for phonon transport into various continuum heat equations using generalized Knudsen numbers, demonstrating that all heat flow regimes—including ballistic, quasiballistic, hydrodynamic, and Fourier—can be described by a single continuum equation derived from eigenmode analysis. This work bridges the gap between microscopic kinetic theory and macroscopic continuum descriptions, providing a comprehensive understanding of heat flow in semiconductors with potential impacts on computational materials science and nanoscale thermal management. Using quantitative examples on 23 technologically important semiconductors, the authors identify previously unrecognized features such as the mutual exclusivity of weakly quasiballistic and hydrodynamic regimes, length-dependent velocity of hydrodynamic second sound, and a characteristic heating length for strongest hydrodynamic second sound.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jun 17, 04:00

**Background**: Heat conduction in semiconductors is governed by phonon transport, described at the microscopic level by the Peierls-Boltzmann equation. Continuum theories like Fourier's law, hydrodynamic heat equations, and quasiballistic models are used to interpret experiments, but they apply only in limiting regimes. The Knudsen number, defined as the ratio of phonon mean free path to a characteristic length scale, determines which regime is relevant. This work generalizes the Knudsen number concept to unify all regimes under a single framework.

## [Quantum effects insufficient to explain bcc metal flow stress](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17954) ⭐️ 8.0/10

This study uses DFT and machine-learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs) to re-evaluate quantum zero-point effects on dislocation glide in bcc metals, finding them far smaller than previous studies suggested and unable to resolve the low-temperature flow stress discrepancy. The findings challenge a long-held assumption in crystal plasticity and reopen a fundamental question about the predictive accuracy of atomistic models for bcc metals, which are critical for structural applications. The work combines density functional theory (DFT) with machine-learned interatomic potentials (MLIPs) to compute quantum zero-point corrections to dislocation migration barriers, showing a contribution of only a few percent to flow stress at low temperatures, versus earlier predictions of tens of percent.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jun 17, 04:00

**Background**: In crystals, atoms exhibit zero-point motion even at absolute zero due to quantum mechanics. Dislocation glide controls plastic deformation in metals, and atomistic simulations often underpredict the low-temperature yield stress of bcc metals. Quantum zero-point effects were proposed to close this gap, but prior studies relied on empirical potentials that may be inaccurate. Machine-learning interatomic potentials trained on DFT data now offer near-DFT accuracy at lower computational cost.
