## [Continuous-wave laser absorption spectroscopy of Th-229 nucleus](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16640) ⭐️ 10.0/10

Researchers demonstrated the first continuous-wave laser excitation of the thorium-229 nuclear transition using a narrow-bandwidth VUV laser with sub-nanowatt power, detecting the resonance via absorption rather than fluorescence. This breakthrough enables a practical solid-state optical nuclear clock with fast signal acquisition, offering unprecedented stability and robustness for fundamental physics and timekeeping applications. The VUV laser source uses three sequential frequency doublings from a 1187 nm diode laser. Absorption spectroscopy characterized two Th centers in a CaF2 crystal, with one center showing an extremely low crystal field gradient of 0.1 V/Å², promising narrow nuclear resonance lines.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jun 18, 04:00

**Background**: The thorium-229 isotope possesses a unique low-energy nuclear transition (around 8.3 eV) that can be excited by laser light, making it a prime candidate for an optical nuclear clock. Previous pulsed laser systems had low efficiency, while continuous-wave excitation with absorption detection dramatically improves signal acquisition speed, essential for clock operation.

## [Spin point group classification of non-relativistic spin splitting in non-collinear magnets](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19254) ⭐️ 9.0/10

This paper presents a comprehensive classification of 1249 spin point groups for non-collinear magnetic structures, including time-reversal symmetry, and provides an online database SPGENPOS on the Bilbao Crystallographic Server. It identifies previously unknown high-order spin splitting types with l=5, 7, and 9. This work is a major theoretical advancement that extends spin group theory to include time-reversal symmetry, enabling the classification of spin splitting in non-collinear magnets crucial for spintronics. The database makes these classifications readily accessible to the research community. The classification covers 1249 nonequivalent spin point groups, revealing that spin splitting is allowed in all groups except those containing the combined operation of time-reversal and space inversion (1'). **Background**: Spin point groups are finite groups whose elements act on both real space and spin space, generalizing magnetic point groups to systems with weak or intermediate spin-orbit coupling. Non-relativistic spin splitting (NRSS) occurs in antiferromagnets without strong spin-orbit coupling, and its classification is important for spintronic applications. The Bilbao Crystallographic Server is an open-access online platform providing crystallographic databases and tools.

## [D-wave Altermagnetism Confirmed in La2O3Mn2Se2 via RIXS](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18731) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Researchers have provided unambiguous experimental evidence of d-wave altermagnetism in the strongly correlated Lieb-lattice magnet La2O3Mn2Se2 using resonant inelastic X-ray scattering (RIXS). They observed d-wave-symmetry circular dichroism in magnetic excitations, which disappears in the paramagnetic phase, confirming that the effect is intrinsic to altermagnetism. This resolves a long-standing debate about whether circular dichroism in RIXS is intrinsic to altermagnetism or an experimental artifact, establishing d-wave altermagnetism as a new fundamental magnetic class. The findings open up avenues for spintronics and functional materials based on altermagnets. The experiments were performed on the Lieb-lattice magnet La2O3Mn2Se2. The d-wave symmetry of the circular dichroism was confirmed via RIXS-operator symmetry analysis and exact-diagonalization calculations, showing it is independent of magnon branch splitting.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jun 18, 04:00

**Background**: Altermagnetism is a recently recognized third fundamental class of magnetic order, distinct from ferromagnetism and antiferromagnetism. It features collinear spins with zero net magnetization but spin-polarized electronic bands due to crystal symmetry. Resonant inelastic X-ray scattering (RIXS) is a spectroscopic technique that probes electronic and magnetic excitations. The Lieb lattice is a geometrically frustrated lattice hosting flat bands and Dirac cones. This study uses these concepts to prove d-wave altermagnetism.

## [Bias in Universal MLIPs and Periodic Fine-Tuning Strategy](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.10159) ⭐️ 9.0/10

This study reveals that universal machine-learned interatomic potentials (uMLIPs) exhibit biases during fine-tuning, and demonstrates that periodic fine-tuning—updating the model at intervals along a single molecular dynamics trajectory—significantly outperforms naive fine-tuning from multiple parallel trajectories for accurate molecular dynamics simulations. These findings are important because they expose a critical flaw in current fine-tuning practices for universal interatomic potentials, and provide a simple yet effective periodic fine-tuning strategy that improves generalizability and accuracy. This directly impacts the reliability of molecular dynamics simulations in computational materials science and chemistry. The uMLIPs achieve an error of about 0.6 kcal/mol on the Matbench Discovery test set, but naive fine-tuning produces constrained datasets that cause models to fail during extrapolation. Periodic fine-tuning yields low-error dynamics and improved robustness, with unphysical behavior related to principal component space and Q-residual analysis serving as a proxy for epistemic uncertainty.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jun 18, 04:00

**Background**: Universal machine-learned interatomic potentials (uMLIPs) are models trained on large quantum-mechanical datasets to predict potential energy surfaces across many elements. They are often fine-tuned on task-specific data to improve accuracy, but the standard approach of generating multiple parallel trajectories can introduce biases. Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations require accurate interatomic potentials to study atomic motion over time. This work compares naive fine-tuning (multiple trajectories) with periodic fine-tuning (single trajectory with periodic updates) and highlights the importance of fine-tuning strategy.

## [Trapped hydrogen atomic explosion triggers crack nucleation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03298) ⭐️ 9.0/10

This study reveals that trapped hydrogen at dislocation cores, not diffusive hydrogen, triggers crack nucleation in tungsten via a two-stage atomic explosion mechanism, overturning the conventional paradigm of hydrogen embrittlement. This work reframes hydrogen embrittlement as a deterministic instability caused by trapped hydrogen, enabling predictive models for hydrogen-induced failure in high-strength metals, crucial for nuclear fusion and hydrogen storage. Using plasma/ion irradiation of tungsten, the authors decoupled crack nucleation from propagation. The mechanism involves hydrogen accumulation at dislocation cores to a critical occupancy, collapsing cohesive strength, then recombination into molecules generating transient inflation pressure that drives crack formation.

rss · arXiv Materials Science · Jun 18, 04:00

**Background**: Hydrogen embrittlement (HE) is a reduction in ductility of metals due to absorbed hydrogen, causing catastrophic failure. For over a century, the prevailing view was that diffusive hydrogen atoms are responsible, and trapping at defects was considered protective. This study reverses that assumption, showing trapped hydrogen actually initiates cracks.

## [Elastic Surface Instability as Topological Phase Transition](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18542) ⭐️ 9.0/10

This paper demonstrates that elastic surface instability in hyperelastic materials under compression is not a mere mechanical bifurcation but fundamentally a topological phase transition, reinterpreting the onset of wrinkles as a transition from trivial to nontrivial topological phase with quantized winding number. This work bridges finite-strain solid mechanics and topological physics, offering a new paradigm for understanding instabilities in soft matter and potentially enabling programmable smart materials through topological design. The authors map the nonlinear geometric frustration onto an algebraic surface impedance matrix via a generalized Stroh formalism, and analytically map the system to a 1D Dirac Hamiltonian where mechanical stretch controls the Dirac mass, yielding a macroscopically localized zero-energy edge state at the instability onset.

rss · arXiv Soft Condensed Matter · Jun 18, 04:00

**Background**: Elastic surface instability, such as the formation of wrinkles on compressed rubber, has traditionally been analyzed using linear stability methods. Topological phase transitions, originally from quantum physics, describe changes in the global topological invariants of a system, such as the winding number. The Stroh formalism is a powerful mathematical tool for solving anisotropic elasticity problems. This study applies these concepts to soft hyperelastic materials undergoing large deformations.

## [NNT extended to nonconserved fields for active matter](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18911) ⭐️ 9.0/10

This paper develops nonequilibrium nucleation theory (NNT) for systems with a nonconserved order parameter, revealing that deviations in the interfacial density profile profoundly alter the nucleation barrier. It provides explicit predictions for models in active matter and population dynamics, validated by numerical studies. This work extends NNT beyond conserved order parameters, enabling theoretical predictions for a broader class of nonequilibrium systems including active matter and ecology. It opens the door to quantitative understanding of phase transitions in systems without detailed balance, such as biological and synthetic active materials. The theory carefully defines the reaction coordinate (droplet radius) to project out interfacial profile deviations, allowing analysis of the nucleation barrier. Explicit NNT predictions are given for population dynamics and active matter models, showing excellent agreement with numerical simulations.

rss · arXiv Soft Condensed Matter · Jun 18, 04:00

**Background**: Classical nucleation theory (CNT) describes how a stable phase emerges from a metastable one through a free-energy barrier. In equilibrium, the barrier arises from a competition between bulk gain and interfacial cost. Previously, nonequilibrium nucleation theory (NNT) was only developed for conserved order parameters (e.g., density in active phase separation). A nonconserved order parameter, such as magnetization or population density in models without local conservation, requires a different theoretical treatment because the order parameter can change locally without transport.

## [Decoupling Strategy Boosts CuInS2 Quantum Dots to Near-Unity Efficiency](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18939) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Researchers introduced a thermodynamic-kinetic decoupling strategy that combines Zn2+ alloying and Ga3+ incorporation to transform defect-tolerant CuInS2 quantum dots into bright, narrowband, photostable single-photon emitters with ~98% quantum yield and g2(0) = 0.06. This work achieves near-unity quantum yield and stable single-photon emission in cadmium-free quantum dots, addressing a major challenge in quantum optics and enabling environmentally benign quantum light sources for applications like luminescent solar concentrators. Zn2+ alloying strains the lattice to suppress native copper vacancies and narrow emission from ~300 meV to ~120 meV, while Ga3+ pins the cation sublattice against Cu+ migration during ZnS shell growth, achieving homogeneous linewidths as low as ~58 meV and external optical efficiency of 12.68% in luminescent solar concentrators.

Single-photon emitters require high purity, brightness, and stability, often demonstrated only in toxic materials at cryogenic temperatures.
